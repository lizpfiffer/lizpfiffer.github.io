Chimpanzee Exhibit

Watercolor and pen. This design incorporates a two-story viewing experience, with resin trees and hidden planters creating the illusion that visitors are looking in on the animals in a “hidden glade”.

Scale Model: Ethiopian Walk-Through

Plywood, balsa, foam, and natural substrates. Final representation of Ethiopian Walk-Through exhibit. Mixed species of Gelada Baboon and Walaya Ibex, both cliff-dwelling species. Featuring bridge crossing for visitors, forced-perspective moat, and extensive keeper access.

  • Date: 2017

Hippo Exhibit

Pen. Design of a lazy-river inspired hippo exhibit to encourage the species’ natural behavior of river walking. Wild hippos travel many miles a day in river beds, and do not spend the majority of their time stationary in stagnant pools. This design incorporates an underwater-viewing area for visitors to see this behavior in action, as well as an overlook for more expansive views.

  • Project: Personal Design
  • Date: 2016
  • Category: Zoo Design

Polar Bear Exhibit

Watercolor and pen. As global warming changes the natural habitat of the polar bear, it becomes increasingly likely that bears would be seen in tundra fields rather than on ice flows. This exhibit shows the polar bear in a modern habitat, without fake ice or white-painted flooring to simulate snow. This exhibit conveys a message of caution and seeks to impart the fragility of our changing landscape.

  • Project: Personal Design
  • Date: 2017
  • Category: Zoo Design

Masterplan

Pen, colored pencil, and Adobe PhotoShop. Finished draft of masterplan layout and exhibit locations. Design focuses on biomes and on smaller, easier to house, and less common species such as servals, tamandua, ocelot, and native pacific northwest flora and fauna. This design also features a rhinocerous conservation center, science discovery center, and eliminates welfare-sensitive species such as elephants and gorillas.

  • Project: Personal Design
  • Date: 2017
  • Category: Zoo Design

Play Area Sketches

Pen. A portion of the Ethiopian Walk-Through exhibit design is a nearby playarea for children that features smooth, curved boulders and encourages outdoor play inspired by the cliff-dwelling and rock-climbing species exhibited in the habitat. The play boulders are set upon recycled rubber turf for safety, are void of sharp edges, and surrounded by benches and viewing areas, creating a public space to both play and watch the animals.

  • Project: Personal Design
  • Date: 2017
  • Category: Zoo Design
